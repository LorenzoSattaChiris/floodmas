## What are the Risk of Flooding from Rivers and Sea products Contents

## products

### Overview

Risk of Flooding from Rivers and Sea (RoFRS) are products that show the chance of
flooding from rivers and the sea to areas of land, taking into account the presence
and condition of flood defences. It is our main way of communicating flood risk from
rivers and the sea to the public through our ‘Check Your Long Term Flood Risk’
service on gov.uk.

### Why they are created

RoFRS has been created to support the public and other decision makers in
understanding, managing and responding to flood risk.

### What they show

RoFRS are probabilistic products that represent risk considering the presence and
condition of flood risk management assets and takes account of the chance of them
overtopping or failing. They show the overall flood risk from rivers and the sea. The
data is presented in flood risk likelihood categories, which indicate the chance of
flooding in any given year.

While flood defences reduce the level of risk, they do not completely remove it. For
example, water can flow over the top of the defence, or they can fail in extreme
weather conditions or if they are in poor condition. As a result, RoFRS may show
that there is risk to areas behind some flood defences.

Risk is displayed as one of four likelihood categories:

```
● High - greater than or equal to 1 in 30 (3.3%) chance of flooding in any year
● Medium – Less than 1 in 30 (3.3%) but greater than or equal to 1 in 100 (1%)
chance of flooding in any given year
● Low – Less than 1 in 100 (1%) but greater than or equal to 1 in 1000 (0.1%)
chance of flooding in any given year
● Very low – less than 1 in 1000 (0.1%) chance of flooding in any given year
```
Climate Change

As well as present day risk of flooding from rivers and the sea, climate change
scenarios have been produced to indicate the predicted impacts of climate change
on future flood risk. The climate change allowances are based on the latest UK
Climate Projections (UKCP18) from the Met Office, using the Representative
Concentration Pathway (RCP) 8.5.


Near-term epochs and central/higher central allowances are being used initially, to
support short and medium-term decisions informed by the highest flood likelihood
projections:

```
● Fluvial: central allowance - 2040 - 2069 ("2050s" epoch)
● Coastal: higher central allowance - 2036 - 2065
```
### RoFRS products

We update and maintain several RoFRS products:

Risk of Flooding from Rivers and Sea

This is our primary dataset showing the flood risk from rivers and the sea,
represented on a 2m resolution grid.

Depths*

This dataset also presents the likelihood of flooding for the following depths:

```
● 0.2m
● 0.3m
● 0.6m
● 0.9m
● 1.2m
```
### Risk of Flooding from Rivers and Sea – Climate Change 1*

A version of the Risk of Flooding from Rivers and Sea dataset showing flood risk
likelihood accounting for climate change, using the scenarios described above, is
also available.

Depths*

This dataset also presents the likelihood of flooding for the following depths:

```
● 0.2m
● 0.3m
● 0.6m
● 0.9m
● 1.2m
```
*Where areas have been retained from a previous version of Risk of Flooding from
Rivers and Sea, this will be a 50m resolution grid and climate change and depth
outputs will not be available. The areas of unavailable data will be indicated within
the dataset layers. Further details on this can be found in How they are created.


Risk of Flooding from Rivers and Sea **–** Properties in Areas at Risk

The RoFRS Properties in Areas at Risk product presents properties in England with
one of the four RoFRS flood risk likelihood categories indicated, based on the
category allocated to the grid cell that the property sits within.

It is delivered as comma-separated values in a plain text format. Individual
addresses are not provided, but Ordnance Survey references are included to enable
the data to be linked to AddressBase and/or the OS MasterMap Topography Layer.

Risk of Flooding from Rivers and Sea **–** Postcodes in Areas at Risk

The RoFRS Postcodes in Areas at Risk product summarises the flood risk likelihood
associated with the properties in each unique postcode in England.

This dataset uses AddressBase and/or the OS MasterMap Topography Layer,
together with Royal Mail postcode data to show how many property receptors in
each postcode fall within each of four flood risk categories, based on the category
allocated to the cell that each property sits within.

It is delivered as comma-separated values in a plain text format.

Risk of Flooding from Rivers and Sea - Key Summary information

The RoFRS Key Summary Information provides a summary of the estimated number
of people and properties in areas of land at risk of flooding from rivers and the sea.
Property counts are also categorised for different property types (residential, non-
residential, unclassified).

It includes total numbers / areas at risk, with an additional breakdown by each of the
four RoFRS flood risk likelihood categories.

Further explanations about the information presented in the spreadsheets, such as
flood likelihood category, property types and how numbers are calculated, are
presented in the first tab of each spreadsheet.

Each set of counts is split by different spatial units:

```
● Country
● ONS Regions
● River Basin District
● RFCC (Regional Flood and Coastal Committee)
● Environment Agency Areas
● LLFA (Lead Local Flood Authority)
● MP constituency
```
It is provided as a series of spreadsheets which provide the following summary
information:


```
● Number of properties in areas at risk from flooding from rivers and the sea
● Number of people in areas at risk from flooding from rivers and the sea
```
## How the Risk of Flooding from Rivers and Sea products are managed

### How they are created

RoFRS is created using a combination of local flood model information and national
flood modelling. These are used to generate the probabilities of flood risk for each
2m grid square of land, with the aim of using the best available flood risk information
in any one location.

For particular areas, sections of a previous (50m resolution) RoFRS dataset have
been retained. These are due to be replaced in the future.

### Update frequency

In the future we plan to publish the data every three months and update it in
locations where new local flood model information is available. Updates on these
timescales can be found on Updates to national flood and coastal erosion risk
information - GOV.UK

## Uses and limitations

### Who uses RoFRS and why

The Environment Agency

The Environment Agency use RoFRS to understand flood risk from rivers and the
sea and take a risk based approach at all levels. It is our primary dataset used to
communicate this flood risk with the public and our partners.

It provides evidence to support investment decisions in the management of our flood
defence assets and also for our Long-term Investment Strategies (LTIS) which set
out the future costs and benefits of managing flood and coastal risks in a range of
different scenarios.

RoFRS is used to help understand local flood risk to secure Partnership Funding
from external contributions for projects.

The public


RoFRS is produced to give the public consistent information about the likelihood of
flooding from rivers and the sea. We want to encourage people living and working in
areas prone to flooding to find out about the flood risk in their area and take
appropriate action.

Our customers and partners

We also make the data available as open data so that customers, partners and
infrastructure owners can use it.

### Suitability and limitations

RoFRS does not show other forms of flooding such as from highway drains,
surface water, sewers, overland flow or rising groundwater.

Flood estimation is not an exact science, and any flood risk assessment needs to be
understood and used in that context.

A confidence category has been assigned, which provides an indicative confidence
level for each spatial cell. However, confidence scores are in development and will
change, therefore should not be used in any decision making at this stage. This is
also not a reflection of how suitable the data is to use on a particular spatial scale.

RoFRS outputs are generally not suitable for property level assessment and
estimate flood risk to an area of land. The method used does not provide information
relating to when the floodwater may be deep enough to start causing damage or
disruption to homes, roads or other infrastructure. Alongside depth information,
additional information on properties (including floor levels) would be required to say
with any confidence whether flooding of a certain depth would enter into a property
and cause damage.

## How to access them

Check Your Long Term Flood Risk service

The Environment Agency presents RoFRS products, including climate change and
depth outputs, on the Check Your Long Term Flood Risk service via GOV.UK where
anyone can access them by entering a postcode or by viewing the national map.

Data Services Platform (DSP)/ data.gov.uk

The RoFRS products are also available in a variety of formats from the Defra Data
Services Platform and data.gov.uk
